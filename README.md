## 🎯 Business Problem

A fintech subscription platform with 440K customers faced significant revenue instability driven by customer attrition. Revenue alone doesn't reveal the full picture — the critical questions are:

- How much revenue is actually at risk from churning customers?
- Which contract structures concentrate the most retention risk?
- What operational factors are driving customers to leave?
- Where should the business intervene first to protect recurring revenue?

---

## 💡 Key Findings

| Area | Finding |
|---|---|
| **Revenue Exposure** | `$135M` — approximately `48.6%` of total platform revenue — was generated by customers who ultimately churned |
| **Contract Risk** | Monthly contracts carry a `100%` churn rate; quarterly and annual plans form the stable revenue core |
| **Churn Driver #1** | Support Calls account for `47%` of model predictive importance — customers are leaving due to unresolved operational friction, not price or disengagement |
| **Churn Driver #2** | Payment Delay is the second strongest predictor (`31%`) — financial friction compounds attrition risk |
| **Retained vs. Churned Spend** | Retained customers spend `~$750` on average vs. `~$541` for churned customers — the business is losing its highest-value users disproportionately |

---

## 🧠 Analytical Approach

1. **Data Cleaning (Python + Pandas)** — Inspected 440K records across 12 variables; converted discrete float columns to integers; removed one row with missing values; validated for duplicates at row and CustomerID level
2. **Customer Segmentation (SQL via DuckDB)** — Analyzed churn rates by Subscription Type and Contract Length; validated anomalous 100% monthly churn rate against raw customer counts
3. **Revenue Analysis (SQL + Python)** — Decomposed total revenue by contract type and subscription tier; identified that average revenue per customer is nearly identical across tiers (~$628–633), meaning volume — not pricing — drives top-line performance
4. **Behavioral Risk Factor Analysis (Python — Scikit-learn)** — Trained a Random Forest Classifier on four behavioral features (Usage Frequency, Support Calls, Payment Delay, Last Interaction); ranked predictors by feature importance
5. **Revenue at Risk Quantification (SQL)** — Calculated the dollar value of revenue generated by churned customers to isolate financial exposure embedded in the current customer base

## 🎯 Strategic Recommendations

**Pillar 1 — Support Audit (Immediate)**
Run a Root Cause Analysis on support tickets to categorize the most frequent issue types and determine whether churn is driven by product bugs, onboarding failures, or service gaps.

**Pillar 2 — Contract Migration Strategy (Short-term)**
Design an incentive program to migrate monthly subscribers to quarterly or annual plans — pricing discounts, loyalty benefits, or bundled value — to reduce revenue volatility.

**Pillar 3 — Predictive Early Warning System (Long-term)**
Integrate the Random Forest model into the CRM workflow to flag high-risk customers before they churn, triggering proactive Customer Success outreach when support or payment delay thresholds are crossed.

## ⚠️ Data Quality & Limitations

- **Synthetic dataset.** The dataset was designed for analytical practice and may not reflect real-world variability in all behavioral patterns — particularly tenure distribution, which appears uniform rather than skewed toward newer customers as is typical in subscription businesses.
- **Observational analysis.** Relationships identified between variables and churn represent correlations, not causal relationships.
- **Model scope.** The Random Forest classifier was trained on four behavioral features. Additional data — support ticket categories, product usage detail, satisfaction scores — would likely improve predictive depth.
- **100% monthly churn rate.** Validated against raw record counts and confirmed as a structural characteristic of this dataset, not a calculation error.

---

## 🎯 Final Takeaway

The analysis consistently points to one actionable conclusion: churn in this platform is not a price or engagement problem — it is an operational friction problem. Support interactions alone explain nearly half of the model's predictive power.

**Reducing support friction and migrating monthly subscribers to longer-term contracts are the two highest-confidence levers available — and the data makes the case from three independent angles: segmentation, revenue quantification, and predictive modeling.**
